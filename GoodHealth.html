The path to SDG 3, "Good Health and Well-being," began with the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs, 2000-2015). While not a standalone health goal, the MDGs targeted critical areas like child and maternal mortality and major diseases like HIV/AIDS, malaria, and tuberculosis. Progress in these areas during the MDG era provided valuable insights and momentum for a more comprehensive health agenda within the subsequent Sustainable Development Goals framework.

SDG 3 broadened the scope of global health ambitions beyond the MDGs. Recognizing health as encompassing physical, mental, and social well-being, it included targets addressing non-communicable diseases, mental health, substance abuse, and environmental health factors. A key focus of SDG 3 became achieving universal health coverage, ensuring equitable access to quality healthcare services, affordable medicines, and vaccines for everyone, marking a more holistic and inclusive approach to global health.

The adoption of the 2030 Agenda in 2015 cemented SDG 3 as a central pillar of sustainable development. Its wide-ranging targets underscore the interconnectedness of health with other development dimensions. The history of SDG 3 reflects a shift from targeted interventions to a more universal and rights-based commitment to ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all ages, acknowledging health as both a foundation and an outcome of sustainable progress.
